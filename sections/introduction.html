I. Introduction

Image geolocation is important for various tasks such as investigative journalism, navigation, and urban analysis [1]. However, in reality, geolocation metadata is frequently missing for reasons like security. This data gap can be filled by image geolocalization, a method of determining the location given an image [2]. In this task, street-view images are particularly valuable by offering rich clues from a ground-level human-centered perspective.

Image geolocalization remains challenging because many real-world scenes are inherently ambiguous, as visually similar locations can exist across distant geographic regions. Recent generative approaches based on diffusion and flow matching address this issue by modeling geolocation as a spatial distribution rather than a single point estimate [8], [9].

In this project, we revisit flow matching-based geolocalization [8] in a local setting restricted to Switzerland. Unlike prior work operating on the Earth sphere \(S^2\) at global scale, we investigate whether a simpler Euclidean formulation in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) is better suited for localized geographic regions. We further explore several conditioning and architectural design choices to better understand their impact on flow matching-based geolocalization.

Our Contributions:

  1. Local Flow Matching Formulation: We adapt generative geolocalization to a Switzerland-focused setting and compare a standard spherical formulation on \(S^2\) with a simpler Euclidean formulation in \(\mathbb{R}^2\).
  2. Conditioning Strategies: We explore multiple conditioning signals for flow matching-based geolocalization, including image embeddings, generated captions, and canton-level classifier priors.
  3. Architecture and Sampling Analysis: We investigate the impact of several design choices, including conditioning normalization, classifier-free guidance strength, number of flow matching steps, sampling strategies, image augmentations.
  4. Comparison with Deterministic Baselines: We compare generative flow matching approaches with simpler deterministic baselines such as coordinate regression and canton classification models.
