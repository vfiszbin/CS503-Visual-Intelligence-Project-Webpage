I. Introduction

Image geolocation is important for various tasks such as emergency responses, navigation, and urban analysis [1]. However, in reality, geolocation metadata is frequently missing for reasons like security. This data gap can be filled by image geolocalization, a method of determining the location given an image [2]. In this task, street-view images are particularly valuable by offering rich clues from a ground-level human-centered perspective.

Previously, image geolocalization often relied on matching a query image against geo-tagged databases or assigning it to predefined spatial partitions, facing hurdles like sparse reference coverage and coarse geo-partitions. Furthermore, these methods are deterministic and often rely on a single modality of information. Recent research has shifted toward generative multimodal learning, which can model spatial ambiguity and integrate auxiliary signals as well. This motivates a systematic study of how different data modalities and modeling choices can be combined to make image geolocalization more accurate and robust.

Our Contributions:

  1. Modality Ablation: How do different modalities contribute to image geolocalization? Specifically, we evaluate whether textual descriptions and potentially aerial imagery improve performance beyond street-view imagery alone, and which signals are most informative.
  2. Methods Comparison: How do deterministic, generative, and hybrid approaches perform in image geolocalization? In particular, we compare flow matching-based generative models with classification and hybrid baselines to understand their strengths and limitations.
  3. Generative Pipeline Design: How do design choices in a generative geolocalization pipeline affect performance? In particular, how do different conditioning strategies and sampling settings influence the quality and sharpness of predicted spatial distributions, and how can these predictions be effectively visualized?
