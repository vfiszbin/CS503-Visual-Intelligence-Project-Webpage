IV. Experiments

Evaluation Metrics

We evaluate the predicted geolocation distributions using several metrics. The experimental results reported in the following sections use the metrics defined below.

Mode median error measures the haversine distance between the ground-truth coordinates and the predicted mode of the sampled distribution. The mode is estimated as the sampled particle with the highest local kernel-density score.

For each sampled coordinate $i$, we compute:

$$ \mathrm{score}_i = \sum_j w_j \exp\left(-\frac{d(i,j)^2}{2h^2}\right) $$

where $d(i,j)$ is the distance between samples, $h$ is a bandwidth parameter, and $w_j$ is the sample weight. In our current sampling setup, the sample weights are uniform. Samples in dense neighborhoods receive higher scores than isolated samples.

Heatmap entropy measures the spatial uncertainty of the predicted distribution. The sampled particles are binned into a latitude/longitude histogram, the histogram mass is normalized into probabilities, and Shannon entropy is computed:

$$ H(p) = -\sum_i p_i \log p_i $$

Lower entropy indicates concentrated predictions, while higher entropy corresponds to more spatially spread predictions.

Canton administrative accuracy measures whether the predicted location falls inside the correct Swiss canton. For each predicted point, we reverse-geocode the coordinates using reverse_geocoder and compare the resulting admin1 label against the dataset ground-truth canton label.

Textual caption conditioning

We explored whether adding a textual caption conditioning could improve the flow matching geolocation model. The original Riemannian Flow Matching architecture conditions only on StreetCLIP image embeddings [8]. We extended this setup by adding a second conditioning branch based on automatically generated image captions.

As shown in the architecture diagram, the image and text embeddings are concatenated before being projected into the flow matching conditioning space. We experimented with several captioning strategies using Qwen3-VL and Gemma4 models. The captions ranged from short free-form geolocation cues to more structured descriptions containing attributes such as terrain, vegetation, road markings, visible script, and infrastructure.

We also tested multiple ablations:

  • image + text conditioning
  • text-only conditioning
  • single-field structured captions
  • targeted VQA-style captions generated from explicit geolocation-related questions

We observed that caption quality and prompting strategy clearly affect performance. However, none of the caption-conditioned variants outperform the image-only baseline. We believe this is because the captions are generated from the same input image already used by the visual encoder, and therefore bring limited additional information. As a result, we did not pursue caption conditioning further.

From global spherical $S^2$ to local planar Euclidean $R^2$ flow matching

The biggest breakthrough came from replacing the original spherical flow matching formulation with a local planar Euclidean one. The original paper performs flow matching on the full Earth sphere ($S^2$) [8]. However, our task is restricted to Switzerland only, where Earth curvature is negligible at this scale. We therefore replaced the Riemannian spherical formulation with a standard Euclidean flow matching model operating on a local ($R^2$) plane.

This change greatly simplified the geometry of the prediction space and produced significantly sharper spatial distributions.

As shown in the figure above, the spherical ($S^2$) formulation tends to spread probability mass over a much larger area, while the local ($R^2$) formulation concentrates predictions much closer to the ground-truth location. Quantitatively, the local Euclidean model improves all main metrics.
