IV. Experiments

Evaluation Metrics

We evaluate the predicted geolocation distributions using several metrics. The experimental results reported in the following sections use the metrics defined below.

  • Mode median error measures the haversine distance between the ground-truth coordinates and the predicted mode of the sampled distribution. The mode is estimated as the sampled particle with the highest local kernel-density score.

    For each sampled coordinate $i$, we compute:

    $$ \mathrm{score}_i = \sum_j w_j \exp\left(-\frac{d(i,j)^2}{2h^2}\right) $$

    where $d(i,j)$ is the distance between samples, $h$ is a bandwidth parameter, and $w_j$ is the sample weight. In our current sampling setup, the sample weights are uniform. Samples in dense neighborhoods receive higher scores than isolated samples.

  • Heatmap entropy measures the spatial uncertainty of the predicted distribution. The sampled particles are binned into a latitude/longitude histogram, the histogram mass is normalized into probabilities, and Shannon entropy is computed:

    $$ H(p) = -\sum_i p_i \log p_i $$

    Lower entropy indicates concentrated predictions, while higher entropy corresponds to more spatially spread predictions.

  • Canton administrative accuracy measures whether the predicted location falls inside the correct Swiss canton. For each predicted point, we reverse-geocode the coordinates using reverse_geocoder and compare the resulting admin1 label against the dataset ground-truth canton label.

Textual caption conditioning

We explored whether adding a textual caption conditioning could improve the flow matching geolocalization model. The original Riemannian Flow Matching architecture conditions only on StreetCLIP image embeddings [8]. We extended this setup by adding a second conditioning branch based on automatically generated image captions.

As shown in the architecture diagram, the image and text embeddings are concatenated before being projected into the flow matching conditioning space. We experimented with several captioning strategies using Qwen3-VL and Gemma4 models. The captions ranged from short free-form geolocation cues to more structured descriptions containing attributes such as terrain, vegetation, road markings, visible script, and infrastructure.

We also tested multiple ablations:

  • image + text conditioning
  • text-only conditioning
  • single-field structured captions
  • targeted VQA-style captions generated from explicit geolocation-related questions

We observed that caption quality and prompting strategy clearly affect performance. However, none of the caption-conditioned variants outperform the image-only baseline. We believe this is because the captions are generated from the same input image already used by the visual encoder, and therefore bring limited additional information. As a result, we did not pursue caption conditioning further.

From global spherical $S^2$ to local planar Euclidean $R^2$ flow matching

The biggest breakthrough came from replacing the original spherical flow matching formulation with a local planar Euclidean one. The original paper performs flow matching on the full Earth sphere ($S^2$) [8]. However, our task is restricted to Switzerland only, where Earth curvature is negligible at this scale. We therefore replaced the Riemannian spherical formulation with a standard Euclidean flow matching model operating on a local ($R^2$) plane.

This change greatly simplified the geometry of the prediction space and produced significantly sharper spatial distributions.

As shown in the figure above, the spherical ($S^2$) formulation tends to spread probability mass over a much larger area, while the local ($R^2$) formulation concentrates predictions much closer to the ground-truth location. Quantitatively, the local Euclidean model improves all main metrics.

The entropy reduction is important: beyond improving localization accuracy, the ($R^2$) formulation also produces much more confident and spatially concentrated predictions.

Conditioning flow matching with classifier priors

We noticed that a simple canton classifier achieved higher canton-level accuracy than our flow matching model. The classifier reached 69.74% canton accuracy, while the tuned ($R^2$) flow matching model1 achieved 54.89%. This suggested that the classifier probabilities contained a strong coarse geographic signal that the flow model was not fully exploiting.

We therefore used the classifier output probability distribution as an additional conditioning signal for flow matching. The 26-dimensional canton probability vector was concatenated with the image embedding before conditioning the flow matching model.

We evaluated three variants:

  • image only: tuned ($R^2$) flow matching baseline
  • image + probs: image embedding concatenated with classifier probabilities
  • probs only: conditioning using only the canton probability vector

The classifier probabilities mainly act as a coarse regional prior. They narrow the plausible geographic area, while the flow matching model still handles the fine-grained localization within the canton. The classifier prior improves overall performance, most specifically the canton accuracy, implying that the flow model could be leveraging the coarse regional signal from the classifier.

Interestingly, the classifier probabilities alone already give strong localization performance. One possible explanation is that, without image information, the flow model learns to place predictions in statistically "average" locations inside likely cantons, which already reduces the median distance significantly. In addition, the full canton probability distribution may contain much richer geographic information than a single predicted label, implicitly encoding neighboring regions and coarse spatial structure across Switzerland. For comparison, a flow matching model trained without any conditioning reaches a much worse median distance of 68.9 km.

Initial sampling shape and size impact

Flow Matching generates locations by transporting samples drawn from an initial distribution. We investigated how the shape and size of this initial sampling region affect localization performance and uncertainty.

Spherical $S^2$ sampling

For the original spherical ($S^2$) model, restricting both training and inference to a small region around Switzerland proved detrimental. The model learned almost zero target velocities, resulting in a degenerate flow that failed to transport samples correctly. In contrast, training on the full sphere while restricting inference to Switzerland remained effective and slightly benefited from circular sampling regions.

Loading S2 sampling results...
Local $R^2$ sampling

For the local ($R^2$) formulation, the effect was different. Very small sampling windows were overly restrictive, while moderately larger windows improved localization accuracy. The best results were obtained with a circular window enlarged by a factor of 16. However, excessively large windows caused the flow to become strongly contractive, collapsing most samples to a single location and reducing uncertainty diversity. Although some enlarged-window variants achieved better results in isolation, subsequent experiments combining other improvements such as LayerNorm and optimal CFG rate ultimately showed that the standard rectangular sampling region remained the strongest overall configuration.

Loading R2 sampling results...

Overall, we see a trade-off between localization accuracy and uncertainty calibration: larger sampling regions can improve point estimates, but may also produce overconfident predictions by collapsing most samples to a single location.

Impact of Classifier-Free Guidance

We evaluated different values of the classifier-free guidance (CFG) rate during inference. CFG combines the conditional velocity predicted from the image with an unconditional velocity learned through condition dropout:

$$ v_{\text{guided}} = v_{\text{cond}} + \lambda \left( v_{\text{cond}} - v_{\text{uncond}} \right) $$

This is the same CFG formulation as in Dufour et al. [8], with $v_{\text{cond}} = \mathrm{model}(x, \gamma, \mathrm{condition})$ and $v_{\text{uncond}} = \mathrm{model}(x, \gamma, \mathrm{zero\_condition})$. Here, $\lambda$ is the CFG rate.

The model conditioned on image features performs substantially better than the unconditioned version, confirming that images provide useful localization information. However, increasing the CFG rate consistently degraded performance. The best results were obtained with $\lambda = 0$, corresponding to pure conditional sampling.

Loading CFG results...

A possible explanation is that, in our local $R^2$ formulation, the prediction space is already restricted to Switzerland and the image-conditioned flow is already strong. Increasing CFG amplifies the image-conditioned correction, making particles move more aggressively at each step. As the CFG rate increases, the predicted distributions become more compact, but this apparent increase in confidence is not reflected in the accuracy metrics. Instead, we observe an overconfident collapse of the particle distribution and worse localization performance.

Number of Particles

To investigate whether the observed collapse was caused by insufficient sampling, we increased the number of inference particles from 64 to 512. The effect was negligible, suggesting that the collapse is driven by the learned flow rather than the sampling density.

Number of Flow Steps

We evaluated the impact of the number of Euler integration steps at inference, ranging from 8 to 128 steps. Performance improves slightly between 8 and 16 steps, but quickly saturates afterwards. Higher step counts provide little improvement in localization accuracy. Interestingly, increasing the number of steps tends to produce more spread-out prediction distributions, resulting in higher uncertainty. We therefore found that 32 steps are already sufficient for this task.

Combining Improvements

The previous experiments identified several promising modifications: local ($R^2$) flow matching, pure conditional sampling ($\text{CFG}=0$), LayerNorm on the conditioning signal, and larger initial sampling regions. However, improvements observed in isolation do not necessarily remain beneficial once combined with other changes.

We then evaluated a number of promising combinations inspired by the previous ablation studies. The goal was not to exhaustively test every possible configuration, but to determine which modifications remained beneficial when combined together. Interestingly, some improvements that looked promising in isolation, such as enlarged initial sampling regions, did not consistently improve the final model. The best overall results were obtained with LayerNorm and pure conditional sampling ($\text{CFG}=0$), which were therefore retained in the final configuration.

After examining the model’s performance across different themes, we found that the frequency with which a scene appears in the training dataset is positively correlated with evaluation performance: scenes that appear more often tend to yield better results. As a result, road scenes perform the best, while railway scenes perform relatively worse because they occur less frequently in the training set. Surprisingly, predictions for snow views are highly accurate, possibly because snowy scenes are geographically concentrated in a limited number of characteristic locations, such as ski resorts and mountainous regions, making them easier for the model to localize.
